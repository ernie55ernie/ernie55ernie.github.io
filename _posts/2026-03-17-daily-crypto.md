## Top Headlines (What & Why it matters)

- **[Risk: Medium] BTC spot ETFs see third straight week of inflows.**
US spot BTC ETFs took in about 767M net last week (Mar 9–13, ET), the strongest run of 2026 so far, with IBIT alone adding ~601M and cumulative IBIT net inflows now above 63B equivalent.[^14][^15][^12]
This sustained bid has helped keep BTC near 74k despite macro uncertainty and supports a “structural demand floor” narrative for large‑cap crypto in the near term.[^13][^2]
- **[Risk: Medium] USDC gaining ground in stablecoin usage.**
USDC’s circulating supply has risen to ~79B (about +5% since the start of 2026 and ~25% since passage of the GENIUS Act), while February stablecoin transfer volume hit a record 1.8T with USDC carrying roughly 70% of that flow versus USDT’s 30%.[^8][^9][^10][^7]
USDT still dominates by outstanding supply (~184B vs ~79B), but USDC is increasingly the preferred rail for institutional payments and regulated settlement, a shift that can influence which venues and chains capture new liquidity.[^9][^10][^11]
- **[Risk: High] US Senate advances housing bill that would ban a Fed retail CBDC until 2030.**
The Senate voted 84–6 in early March to advance the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act, whose Title X currently bars the Federal Reserve from issuing or indirectly facilitating a retail CBDC or “substantially similar” digital asset until Dec 31, 2030 (cloture vote; further steps still required).[^16][^17][^18][^19]
The White House has signaled support, so if the House agrees on final language, this would effectively lock in a multi‑year bias toward privately issued stablecoins over a US digital dollar, with material implications for long‑run payment‑token competition.[^20][^21][^22][^23]
- **[Risk: Medium] SEC & CFTC move to coordinated “Project Crypto” rulebook.**
SEC Chair Atkins and CFTC Chair Selig announced on Jan 30 that Project Crypto will proceed as a joint initiative to harmonize federal oversight of digital‑asset markets, including a shared token taxonomy, aligned definitions, and potential joint rulemakings.[^24][^25][^26][^27]
March legal and compliance updates continue to frame this as a shift from ad‑hoc enforcement toward a more unified, forward‑looking regulatory architecture, which should gradually reduce jurisdictional uncertainty for exchanges and token issuers.[^28][^27][^29]
- **[Risk: Medium] SEC exam priorities drop crypto as a standalone focus.**
The SEC Division of Examinations’ 2026 priorities, released in November 2025, omit cryptoassets as a separate exam pillar for the first time since 2018, even as the agency continues to emphasize fiduciary duty, custody, cybersecurity and AI‑related risks.[^30][^31][^32][^33]
Law‑firm analyses interpret this as a tone shift—crypto is no longer flagged as a special systemic risk—though staff are still expected to scrutinize crypto activities under general conduct and custody rules, so this is more de‑emphasis than deregulation.[^34][^35][^32]
- **[Risk: Medium] Venus Protocol suffers 3.7M exploit via Thena (THE) collateral attack.**
On Mar 14–15 (approx., UTC not specified), a long‑running attacker who had accumulated ~84% of the THE supply cap on Venus used a “donation attack” to bypass supply limits, manipulated THE’s on‑chain price, and borrowed ~3.7M in CAKE, USDC, BNB and BTC before markets were paused, leaving ~2–2.15M in bad debt.[^36][^37][^38][^39][^40]
The incident, highlighted in several risk and news briefings, underscores structural weaknesses in Compound‑forked lending protocols where governance caps are not strictly enforced at the contract level.[^41][^42][^43]

## Regulation / Law

- **[Risk: High] US retail CBDC effectively on hold pending housing bill outcome.**
The Senate’s 84–6 cloture vote on the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act embeds a temporary ban on a Fed retail CBDC and “substantially similar” digital instruments until Dec 31, 2030, with the White House explicitly endorsing the restriction; the bill still faces amendment votes, House–Senate reconciliation, and final passage before becoming law.[^17][^21][^18][^22][^19][^50][^20][^16]
- **[Risk: Medium] SEC–CFTC “Project Crypto” signals coordinated US market‑structure rules.**
Official speeches and law‑firm summaries of the Jan 29 joint SEC–CFTC meeting describe Project Crypto as a shared framework to align digital‑asset classifications, reduce duplicative registrations, and support joint rulemaking, with a memorandum of understanding envisaged.[^25][^26][^27][^24]
March regulatory roundups emphasize that staff are now working toward a unified taxonomy and surveillance approach ahead of potential Congressional market‑structure legislation, which could materially change how spot platforms and derivative venues are supervised.[^51][^52][^28]
- **[Risk: Medium] MiCA moves toward full EU‑wide enforcement through 2026.**
ESMA’s MiCA portal and March commentary highlight that CASPs are in the final phase of national grandfathering windows ahead of an EU‑wide deadline around Jul 1, 2026, after which unauthorised providers must cease regulated crypto‑asset services in the EU.[^53][^54][^55]
Member‑state transitional periods differ, but the direction is clear: 2026 is framed as a “filter year” in which non‑compliant EU platforms will be forced out or consolidated, tightening the regulatory perimeter for EUR‑facing order flow.[^56][^55]

## Research / White Papers

- **[Risk: Medium] Glassnode Week On‑Chain (Week 10, “Resilient in the Face of War”).**
The Mar 10 note characterizes BTC as range‑bound in roughly 62.8k–72.6k, with front‑end implied vol compressing faster than longer tenors and ETF inflows helping stabilize spot conditions.[^57][^58]
Glassnode stresses that a dense unresolved supply zone above ~98–100k remains a major overhang, implying that a sustained breakout would require a meaningful acceleration in net demand beyond current ETF‑driven flows.[^29]
- **[Risk: Medium] Nominis February 2026 Web3 security report.**
The Mar 14 report estimates ~49.3M in February crypto losses versus ~385M in January, with a large Step Finance treasury compromise (~27–40M), IoTeX cross‑chain infrastructure issues, YieldBlox oracle/policy exploits, and several sophisticated phishing/approval‑drain scams.[^59][^60][^61][^41]
The authors highlight a tilt away from pure contract bugs toward infrastructure compromises and social‑engineering vectors (address‑poisoning, malicious allowance approvals), indicating that user‑level operational security and key management remain central risk factors.
- **[Risk: Medium] Chainalysis 2026 Crypto Crime Report (intro & sanctions chapter).**
Chainalysis’ January–March releases estimate that illicit addresses received at least 154B of crypto in 2025 (+162% YoY), driven largely by a 694% surge in value flowing to sanctioned entities, with stablecoins comprising roughly 84% of illicit volume and state‑linked actors like Russia, Iran and North Korea playing outsized roles.[^62][^63][^64][^65]
This reinforces that regulatory and sanctions risk is increasingly tied to stablecoin rails and that nation‑state activity is now a core driver of headline “crypto crime” statistics.

## Security Incidents

- **[Risk: Medium] Venus Protocol – Thena (THE) supply‑cap / donation exploit (~3.7M).**
Between late 2025 and Mar 15 2026 (date only; time/zone not specified), an attacker gradually accumulated ~84% of THE’s supply cap on Venus, then bypassed the cap by transferring tokens directly to the vTHE contract (“donation attack”), inflated THE’s price from roughly 0.26–0.27 to the 0.56–5 range on thin liquidity, and borrowed ~3.7M in CAKE, USDC, BNB and BTC, leaving ~2–2.15M in protocol bad debt.[^37][^38][^39][^40][^36]
Venus has paused THE‑related markets and is working with its risk manager Allez Labs on remediation; there is no evidence so far of contagion to other major BNB Chain protocols, but the pattern echoes known weaknesses in Compound‑style lending markets where supply caps are not enforced at the contract level.[^42][^43][^39][^36]
- **[Risk: Low] No other large new on‑chain exploits in last 7 days; February trend still instructive.**
Outside Venus, no comparably sized hacks have been confirmed in the last week, with recent security dashboards instead emphasizing February’s relatively low aggregate losses (~26–50M) versus January, but concentrated in a handful of protocol‑ and infrastructure‑level incidents (YieldBlox, IoTeX, Step Finance, etc.).[^61][^59][^41]
