## Market Overview

Total crypto market cap is roughly 2.4–2.5 trillion USD, down about 1.5–2% over the last 24 hours as the post-bounce pullback continues. Bitcoin trades near 70,000–71,000 USD, with CoinCodex showing 70,451 USD and a −2.7% 24h move and dominance around 58.7%. Ethereum is around 2,070 USD, off roughly 1–2% on the day and holding just above the 2,000 USD pivot.[^2][^3][^4][^1]

On your watchlist, SOL is about 87–89 USD, down ~1.3% on March 6, while BNB is around 650 USD and marginally green (~+0.3–0.4% 24h), making it one of the few majors outperforming BTC. XRP sits near 1.40 USD, broadly flat versus earlier in the week. Glassnode notes BTC is consolidating in a 60–72k “defensive” band with fading spot demand and cautious derivatives positioning, consistent with today’s modest risk-off tone.[^5][^6][^7][^8][^9][^10][^11]

Derivatives are a key driver: Deribit sees a 2.68 billion USD options expiry today at 08:00 UTC (about 2.2 billion in BTC notional, 397 million in ETH), with max pain around 69,000 USD for BTC, contributing to choppy price action and a 4.76% drop in total crypto open interest over 24h. CoinGlass data cited by crypto.news shows ~252 million USD in liquidations in the last day, ~167.5 million from longs, underscoring a leverage flush rather than spot panic. CryptoQuant and MEXC commentary highlight over 30,000 BTC flowing out of derivatives venues as price approached 72,900 and structurally low funding, pointing to a de-levered, options‑driven market.[^12][^13][^2]

US spot BTC ETFs remain a central narrative: early March saw a strong 458.2 million USD net inflow day led by BlackRock’s IBIT (263.2m), with FBTC adding 94.8m and ARKB 5.7m. This week, flows turned more mixed—SoSoValue/Farside data show 225.2m USD net inflows on March 4 but an estimated −227.9m USD net outflow on March 5, including −88.7m from IBIT, −48m from FBTC, and −22.7m from ARKB. Despite the volatility, cumulative March ETF inflows are back positive and approaching 700–800m USD, helping frame BTC’s resilience near 70k.[^14][^15][^16][^17][^18]

Stablecoins remain steady: Tether’s USDT has a market cap around 184 billion USD with 24h volume near 86–89 billion and continues to trade essentially at peg. USDC’s market cap is in the low‑70 billions, with recent on-chain data showing a large >900m USD USDC net inflow to Binance on March 3, signalling growing USD liquidity “buying power” on exchanges even as prices consolidate.[^19][^20][^21][^22]

## Top Headlines

1. **Deribit 2.68B options expiry heightens short‑term vol (Risk: Medium).**
Deribit faces expiry of roughly 2.2B USD in BTC options (~32,000 contracts, max pain ~69k) and ~397M USD in ETH options today at 08:00 UTC, with total crypto open interest down 4.76% into the event. This is pressuring spot as traders rebalance around key strikes and helps explain elevated liquidations despite only modest spot moves.[^2]
2. **Bitcoin ETF flows swing from strong inflows to fresh outflows (Risk: Medium).**
After a 458.2m USD net inflow day led by IBIT (263.2m) and solid contributions from FBTC and ARKB, US spot BTC ETFs logged 225.2m in net inflows on Tuesday, then an estimated −227.9m net outflow on March 5, again with IBIT and FBTC in focus. The whipsaw shows institutions are re‑engaging but still highly tactical, limiting how much ETF demand can offset macro and options‑driven selling day‑to‑day.[^15][^16][^17][^18][^14]
3. **Trump publicly backs pro‑stablecoin legislation, boosting Coinbase and sector sentiment (Risk: Medium).**
President Trump met privately with Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong, then posted on Truth Social urging banks to “strike a favorable deal with the Crypto Industry” and backing legislation like the GENIUS/CLARITY Acts that would allow yield‑bearing stablecoin products, helping COIN rally 12–15% and signalling a friendlier executive‑branch tone. This alignment with Coinbase on stablecoin yields escalates the political fight with large banks and could accelerate Congressional work on US crypto market structure and stablecoin rules.[^23][^24][^25][^26]
4. **SEC crypto rulemaking enters White House review; exam priorities de‑emphasize crypto (Risk: High for structure, supportive for sentiment).**
The SEC’s draft rule “Application of the Federal Securities Laws to Certain Types of Crypto Assets and Certain Transactions Involving Crypto Assets” has been sent to the White House’s OIRA for review, signalling imminent interpretive guidance on token taxonomy and market structure. In parallel, analyses of the SEC’s 2026 exam priorities and the new joint “Project Crypto” with the CFTC show crypto removed as a standalone top‑risk category, suggesting a pivot from one‑off enforcement toward a coordinated framework—important for perceived US regulatory overhang even though enforcement risk remains.[^27][^28][^29]
5. **Solv Protocol hacked for ~2.7M USD in BTCFi exploit; 10% bounty offered (Risk: High for BTC DeFi niche).**
Solv Protocol disclosed a 2.7M USD loss after an attacker abused a double‑minting bug in a BRO vault, inflating holdings via 22 transactions into ~567M BRO, swapping into ~38 SolvBTC, and draining the vault, though fewer than 10 users and one contract were affected. The team has offered a 10% (~270k USD) bounty to recover funds and stresses other vaults remain safe; the incident hits the narrative around Bitcoin‑backed DeFi just as BTCFi is trying to attract more institutional capital.[^30][^31]

## Exchanges

Binance continues aggressive product expansion: it just added new spot pairs AVAX/U, LINK/U, LTC/U, PAXG/U, and ZEC/U with a zero‑maker‑fee campaign starting March 5 at 11:00 UTC to deepen liquidity in USD‑stable pairs. Binance is also adding these pairs to full‑margin leverage at 18:00 (UTC+8) on March 5 and listing new tokens like Fabric Protocol (ROBO) and OPN via Launchpool, keeping its listing calendar busy despite regulatory scrutiny.[^32][^33][^34][^35]

Derivatives venues are adjusting around OPN: Bybit will convert its OPNUSDT pre‑market perpetual contract to a standard perpetual around March 5, 11:45 UTC, aligning with Binance’s OPN spot listing and opening cleaner basis trading. No major, confirmed outages or systemic incidents have been reported on Binance, Coinbase, OKX, Kraken, Bybit, or Deribit in the last 24–48 hours beyond routine upgrades and user‑level complaints.[^36][^37][^38][^32]

## Regulation

In the US, the SEC’s crypto rule plan now under OIRA review could soon produce guidance on when various tokens are treated as securities, intersecting with Congress’s broader market‑structure bills and the administration’s more crypto‑friendly rhetoric. Separately, the SEC’s Crypto Task Force has begun publishing written input, including a March 5 Coin Center letter pressing for a formal safe harbor for decentralized networks and tokenized securities infrastructure, highlighting industry focus on predictable registration paths rather than case‑by‑case relief.[^29][^39][^26]

In the EU, MiCA is in the implementation and enforcement phase rather than announcing new measures this week; ESMA and national regulators continue to stress that all CASPs must be fully authorized before transitional periods end by July 1, 2026, or wind down EU operations. In the UK, existing FCA consultations on the new cryptoasset regime, SM&CR extension, and stablecoin framework are ongoing through Q1 2026, but there have been no major rule changes in the last seven days beyond previously signaled timelines toward a 2027 go‑live.[^40][^41][^42][^43][^44]

In Asia, Hong Kong is working toward a broader licensing regime for virtual asset dealing, custody, and advisory services—alongside SFC guidance from February that will allow licensed VATPs to offer virtual‑asset perpetual contracts under defined risk controls—but no new circulars have dropped in the last week. I could not find any material new crypto‑specific announcements from MAS (Singapore), Japan’s FSA, or Taiwan’s FSC during the last seven days; previously announced prudential and virtual‑asset frameworks remain the main reference points.[^45][^46][^47][^48][^49]

## Research

Glassnode’s latest Week On‑chain (March 3) describes BTC as trapped between key on‑chain cost‑basis bands, with short‑term holders sitting near breakeven and broader holder behaviour still defensive—spot and ETF flows have stabilized but remain weak, and derivatives leverage is low with largely neutral funding. A separate Glassnode‑based analysis highlighted that only about 57% of BTC supply is in profit at current prices around 70k, a level historically associated with early‑stage bear regimes (2018/2022) rather than euphoric tops, implying the on‑chain backdrop is still fragile.[^10][^50][^11]

A March 3 institutional deep‑dive on Coinbase (COIN) frames 2026 as an “infrastructure‑led” phase: Coinbase is portrayed as a hybrid exchange‑custodian‑software platform, with 2025 subscription and services revenue at 2.8B USD (40% of total) and Base L2 plus RWA tokenization and AI‑agent activity on Base driving its long‑term thesis. The same piece emphasizes that Coinbase now custodies over 80% of US spot BTC ETF assets and is a primary beneficiary of institutional flows, underlining how ETF and RWA narratives tie back into the exchange and L2 stack rather than just underlying coins.[^51]

## Security

The most acute new incident is the Solv Protocol exploit in the BTCFi space: a double‑minting bug in one BRO vault let an attacker repeatedly inflate their position and withdraw ~38 SolvBTC (about 2.7M USD), though Solv says the damage is contained to a single vault and <10 addresses and has offered a 10% bounty for cooperation. This follows February’s IoTeX ioTube bridge hack (8M+ USD lost via a compromised validator key) and CrossCurve’s ~3M USD cross‑chain exploit, both of which highlight a shift from pure code bugs toward key/operational compromises and cross‑chain messaging flaws.[^52][^53][^54][^31][^55][^30]

Aggregate hack data show early‑2026 losses are lower but still meaningful: PeckShield and MEXC cite roughly 112.5M USD in confirmed protocol‑level losses in the first two months, with January heavily skewed by a handful of large DeFi incidents and February dropping to ~26–38M USD—the lowest monthly figure since March 2025 but still dominated by a few bridge and DeFi exploits. TRM Labs’ 2026 Crypto Crime Report finds that in 2025, about 2.9B USD was stolen, with a majority coming from infrastructure/key attacks (like Bybit’s 1.46B breach) rather than traditional smart‑contract bugs, a pattern that appears to be continuing into 2026.[^56][^57][^58][^59][^60]

## What to Watch (24–72h)

- How BTC and ETH trade around the Deribit 2.68B USD options expiry—particularly BTC near the 69k max‑pain level—and whether OI rebuilds with directional conviction or stays depressed.[^2]
- Subsequent US spot BTC ETF flows after the −227.9m USD net outflow on March 5; watch whether IBIT/FBTC/ARKB resume net inflows or see continued redemptions despite improving Coinbase premium and early‑March strength.[^61][^16][^17][^14]
- Any publication of the SEC’s crypto rule guidance post‑OIRA review and further activity from the SEC Crypto Task Force and Senate Banking Committee on market‑structure bills, especially given Trump’s new public push around stablecoins and CLARITY‑style legislation.[^39][^62][^26][^29][^23]
- Updates from Solv Protocol on negotiations or partial recovery of the 2.7M USD exploit and any spill‑over to other BTCFi protocols or centralized venues listing Solv‑related instruments.[^31][^30]
- Liquidity and volatility around Binance’s new listings (OPN, ROBO, new AVAX/LINK/LTC/PAXG/ZEC pairs) and Bybit’s OPNUSDT perpetual conversion, which could create short‑term dislocations or basis trades in those names.[^33][^34][^35][^32][^36]

Overall confidence: medium‑high, with good coverage of spot/derivatives, ETFs, regulation, and security, but some price and flow figures vary slightly by source snapshot.
